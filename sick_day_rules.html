Sick Day Rules for Diabetes

Essential Medication Management During Illness

⚠️ Critical Information: Protect Your Kidneys

When you are sick with vomiting, diarrhea, or high fever and cannot maintain adequate hydration, certain medications must be temporarily stopped to prevent serious kidney damage. This is known as the "Sick Day Rule" and is essential for your safety.1,2,3

What Are the Sick Day Rules?

The Sick Day Rules are a set of guidelines designed to protect your kidneys during acute illness. When you're sick and losing fluids through vomiting, diarrhea, or fever, your body becomes dehydrated. Certain medications that are normally safe can become dangerous when you're dehydrated because they put extra stress on your kidneys.1,2

Your kidneys are responsible for filtering waste from your blood and maintaining the right balance of fluids and minerals in your body. When you're dehydrated, blood flow to your kidneys decreases. Some medications can worsen this situation, potentially causing acute kidney injury â€" a serious and sometimes irreversible condition.2,3,4

Why Is This Important?

Studies have shown that continuing certain medications during dehydrating illnesses significantly increases the risk of acute kidney injury. By following the Sick Day Rules, you can reduce your risk of kidney damage by up to 50%.3,5

When Should I Follow the Sick Day Rules?

You should stop your medications and follow these rules if you experience any of the following:1,2,6

Sick Day Rule Triggers

Additional Situations That Require Caution

  • Reduced food and fluid intake: If you haven't been able to eat or drink normally for 24 hours
  • Signs of dehydration: Dark urine, dizziness when standing, dry mouth, decreased urination
  • Excessive sweating: From fever, heat, or strenuous activity while ill
  • Before planned procedures: Always inform your healthcare team about these medications before surgery or procedures requiring fasting6

Which Medications Should I Stop?

During sick days, you must temporarily pause four main categories of medications. These are sometimes remembered by the acronym "SADMANS" (Sick day rules for: Sulphonylureas, ACE-inhibitors/ARBs, Diuretics, Metformin, SGLT2 inhibitors, NSAIDs).1,2,3

1. Blood Pressure Medications (ACE Inhibitors and ARBs)

How to identify: These medications typically end in -pril or -sartan

Common examples:

  • ACE Inhibitors (-pril): Ramipril, Lisinopril, Enalapril, Perindopril, Quinapril
  • ARBs (-sartan): Losartan, Valsartan, Candesartan, Irbesartan, Telmisartan

Why stop? These medications lower blood pressure by relaxing blood vessels. When you're dehydrated, they can cause blood pressure to drop too low and reduce blood flow to the kidneys, potentially causing acute kidney injury.2,4,7

2. Diuretics (Water Pills)

Common examples:

  • Furosemide (Lasix)
  • Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ)
  • Bumetanide
  • Indapamide
  • Chlorthalidone
  • Spironolactone (Aldactone)

Why stop? Diuretics increase urine production to remove excess fluid. When you're already dehydrated from illness, continuing diuretics can worsen dehydration and harm your kidneys.2,4

3. Diabetes Medications

Metformin:

  • Metformin, Glucophage
  • Combination products: Janumet, Glucovance, others

Why stop? When you're dehydrated and your kidneys aren't working optimally, metformin can build up in your system and cause a rare but serious condition called lactic acidosis.2,8

SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins):

  • Empagliflozin (Jardiance)
  • Dapagliflozin (Forxiga)
  • Canagliflozin (Invokana)
  • Ertugliflozin (Steglatro)

Why stop? These medications cause you to lose glucose and fluid through urine, which can worsen dehydration and increase the risk of diabetic ketoacidosis, especially when you're not eating normally.2,9

Sulphonylureas:

  • Gliclazide (Diamicron)
  • Glyburide (Glibenclamide)
  • Glipizide
  • Glimepiride

Why stop? When you're not eating normally, these medications can cause dangerously low blood sugar levels (hypoglycemia).2

GLP-1 Agonists (Injectable medications):

  • Semaglutide (Ozempic)
  • Dulaglutide (Trulicity)
  • Liraglutide (Victoza)

Why stop? These medications slow stomach emptying and can worsen nausea and vomiting during illness. They also reduce appetite when you may already have difficulty eating.10

4. Anti-Inflammatory Pain Relievers (NSAIDs)

Common examples:

  • Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)
  • Naproxen (Aleve, Naprosyn)
  • Diclofenac (Voltaren)
  • Celecoxib (Celebrex)
  • Indomethacin
  • Ketorolac

Why stop? NSAIDs reduce blood flow to the kidneys. Combined with dehydration, this significantly increases the risk of acute kidney injury.2,4,11

✓ SAFE ALTERNATIVE: Acetaminophen (Tylenol, Paracetamol) is safe to use for pain and fever during sick days as it does not affect kidney function in the same way.2

What Medications Should I Continue?

It's equally important to know which medications you should continue taking during illness:1,2

Continue These Medications:

  • Insulin: If you take insulin, continue it but may need dose adjustments. Contact your healthcare provider for guidance2,12
  • Acetaminophen/Tylenol: Safe for pain and fever relief
  • Antibiotics: If prescribed for the current illness
  • Thyroid medications
  • Anti-seizure medications
  • Heart rhythm medications
  • Blood thinners (unless directed otherwise by your doctor)
  • Steroids (prednisone, etc.) â€" never stop these suddenly
  • Psychiatric medications (antidepressants, antipsychotics, etc.)

When in doubt, contact your healthcare provider or pharmacist before stopping any medication.

When Should I Restart My Medications?

This is a critical question, and the timing is important for your safety.1,2,6

Do NOT Restart Immediately

Many people make the mistake of restarting their medications as soon as they feel slightly better or when vomiting stops. This can be dangerous because your body needs time to fully rehydrate and for your kidney function to normalize.2,6

Safe Restart Checklist

You can restart your medications when ALL of the following criteria are met:

You have been eating and drinking normally for 24 to 48 hours
No vomiting for at least 24 hours
No diarrhea, or diarrhea has resolved to near-normal bowel movements
Fever has resolved
You are well-hydrated (passing normal amounts of light-colored urine)
You feel your energy returning to normal

When to Seek Medical Advice Before Restarting

Contact your healthcare provider or pharmacist before restarting if:2,6

  • You were ill for more than 3 days
  • You have ongoing kidney disease
  • You're over 65 years old
  • You have heart failure
  • You're unsure about your hydration status
  • You experienced dizziness or lightheadedness
  • You notice changes in your urine output or color

Additional Sick Day Management Tips

Monitoring Your Condition

During illness, monitor the following:2,12

  • Blood glucose: Check more frequently if you have diabetes (every 2-4 hours). Illness typically raises blood sugar even if you're eating less
  • Ketones: If you have type 1 diabetes, check urine or blood ketones if blood glucose is above 14 mmol/L (250 mg/dL)
  • Hydration status: Monitor urine color and frequency
  • Temperature: Track fever patterns
  • Symptoms: Note changes in nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea

Staying Hydrated

Hydration is crucial during sick days:2,13

  • Aim for small, frequent sips of fluid rather than large amounts at once
  • Good fluid options: water, diluted juice, clear broth, electrolyte solutions
  • If you have diabetes and are vomiting: alternate between sugar-free and regular fluids to maintain blood glucose
  • Avoid caffeinated and alcoholic beverages as they can worsen dehydration
  • Ice chips or popsicles can help if you're having trouble keeping fluids down

Nutrition During Illness

When you're sick:2,12

  • Try to eat small amounts of easily digestible foods
  • Good options: crackers, toast, rice, applesauce, bananas
  • If you have diabetes: try to consume some carbohydrates to prevent low blood sugar, especially if taking insulin
  • Don't force yourself to eat large meals; small frequent snacks are better

⚠️ When to Seek Emergency Care

Call 911 or go to the emergency department if you experience:

  • Persistent vomiting for more than 24 hours
  • Signs of severe dehydration: extreme thirst, very dark urine or no urine, dizziness, confusion
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Blood in vomit or stool
  • High fever (>39.4°C/103°F) that doesn't respond to acetaminophen
  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain
  • For people with diabetes: blood glucose consistently above 20 mmol/L (360 mg/dL) or ketones in urine/blood
  • Altered mental status or extreme weakness

Creating Your Personal Sick Day Plan

Work with your healthcare team to create a personalized sick day plan. This should include:1,2

Component Details
Medication List Complete list of all your medications with clear marking of which to stop during sick days
Contact Information Phone numbers for your doctor, pharmacist, and after-hours care
Monitoring Plan How often to check blood sugar, when to check ketones (if applicable)
Insulin Adjustments If you take insulin, specific instructions for dose adjustments during illness
Warning Signs Symptoms that require immediate medical attention specific to your conditions
Emergency Contacts Family members or friends who can help in an emergency

Preparing for Sick Days

Be prepared before illness strikes:

  • Keep a written list of medications to stop on sick days posted on your refrigerator or in your medication storage area
  • Ensure family members know about the sick day rules
  • Stock acetaminophen/Tylenol for fever and pain relief
  • Keep electrolyte solutions or clear fluids on hand
  • Have a thermometer available
  • If you have diabetes, keep glucose monitoring supplies readily accessible and know how to check ketones
  • Save your healthcare provider's contact information in your phone

Key Takeaways

Remember These Essential Points:

  1. STOP medications during vomiting, diarrhea, or fever with poor fluid intake: ACE inhibitors/ARBs (-pril/-sartan), diuretics, metformin, SGLT2 inhibitors, sulphonylureas, GLP-1 agonists, and NSAIDs
  2. SWITCH to Tylenol instead of ibuprofen or naproxen for pain/fever relief
  3. CONTINUE insulin and other essential medications (may need dose adjustments â€" contact your provider)
  4. WAIT 24-48 hours after you're eating and drinking normally before restarting medications
  5. CONTACT your healthcare provider if you're unsure about any medication decisions
  6. SEEK emergency care for severe symptoms or prolonged illness

Questions to Ask Your Healthcare Team

  • What should I do with my insulin doses if I'm sick?
  • At what point should I call you if I'm ill?
  • Are there any other medications specific to my conditions that I should stop?
  • Can we create a written sick day action plan together?
