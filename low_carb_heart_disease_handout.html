Low-Carbohydrate Diets and Heart Disease Risk

Understanding the Complete Picture Beyond LDL Cholesterol

The Purpose of This Handout

You may have heard conflicting advice about low-carbohydrate diets and heart health. Some doctors worry that these diets might harm your heart, while others recommend them for improving metabolic health. This handout will help you understand the nuance of this discussion and make an informed decision with your healthcare team.

The Controversy: Why Do Some Doctors Discourage Low-Carb Diets?

If you're considering or already following a low-carbohydrate or ketogenic diet, you may have encountered resistance from healthcare providers, particularly cardiologists. This concern usually centers on one thing: LDL cholesterol (often called "bad cholesterol").1,2

Many doctors have been trained to focus heavily on LDL cholesterol as the primary marker of heart disease risk. When some people adopt low-carbohydrate diets, their LDL cholesterol may rise. This triggers concern, even when other markers are improving dramatically.3,4

The Traditional View

For decades, the medical establishment has used LDL cholesterol as the primary target for heart disease prevention. Cardiologists are taught that lower LDL is always better, and anything that raises LDL is concerning. This single-minded focus, while well-intentioned, can miss important parts of the picture.1,2

The Missing Piece: Insulin Resistance and Heart Disease

What many doctors overlook is that insulin resistance is a powerful driver of cardiovascular disease — often more important than LDL cholesterol alone.5,6,7

Insulin resistance creates a cascade of problems that directly damage your blood vessels:6,7,8

  • Chronic inflammation: Insulin resistance causes ongoing inflammation in your blood vessel walls
  • High triglycerides: Excess blood fats that damage arteries
  • Low HDL cholesterol: Reduced levels of protective "good" cholesterol
  • High blood pressure: Increased strain on blood vessels
  • Abnormal blood clotting: Increased risk of heart attacks and strokes
  • Damaging LDL particles: Small, dense LDL particles that easily penetrate artery walls

Here's the critical point: a low-carbohydrate diet directly addresses insulin resistance, which may be the root cause of your cardiovascular risk.9,10,11

Why LDL Cholesterol Alone Doesn't Tell the Full Story

Not All LDL Particles Are Equal

This is perhaps the most important concept to understand: the size and type of your LDL particles matters more than the total amount.12,13,14

LDL Particle Size: The Key Difference

Left: In people with insulin resistance, LDL particles are small and dense—they easily penetrate artery walls and cause inflammation. Right: In metabolically healthy people, LDL particles are large and fluffy—they're less likely to cause problems even at higher cholesterol levels.

Pattern A vs. Pattern B

Scientists have identified two main patterns of LDL particles:12,13,14

  • Pattern A (Large, Fluffy LDL): These particles are too large to easily squeeze between the cells lining your arteries. They're less likely to become oxidized (damaged) and cause inflammation. This pattern is associated with metabolically healthy individuals.
  • Pattern B (Small, Dense LDL): These smaller particles easily penetrate the artery wall where they become trapped, oxidized, and trigger inflammation. This pattern is strongly linked to insulin resistance and significantly increases heart disease risk.

The Critical Insight

Two people can have the exact same LDL cholesterol number (say, 4.7 mmol/L), but one person with large fluffy particles may have very low cardiovascular risk, while another with small dense particles may have high risk. The number alone doesn't tell you which pattern you have.13,14,15

The Problem with Framingham Risk Scores

Traditional cardiovascular risk calculators (like the Framingham Risk Score) were developed decades ago and rely heavily on total and LDL cholesterol levels. However, these calculators have significant limitations:15,16

  • In metabolically unhealthy people: The Framingham score underestimates risk because it doesn't account for small, dense LDL particles. Your calculated risk may look moderate, but your actual risk is much higher.
  • In metabolically healthy people: The Framingham score overestimates risk because it treats all LDL as equally dangerous. Your calculated risk may look high, but your actual risk is lower.

The Triglyceride to HDL Ratio: A Better Predictor

While many doctors focus exclusively on LDL cholesterol, research shows that the ratio of triglycerides to HDL cholesterol is often a much better predictor of heart disease risk.17,18,19

Why This Ratio Matters

Your triglyceride to HDL ratio is a powerful indicator of:17,18

  • Insulin resistance
  • LDL particle size (lower ratio = larger particles)
  • Metabolic health
  • Cardiovascular disease risk
Trig:HDL Ratio What It Means LDL Particle Pattern Risk Level
Below 2:1 Excellent insulin sensitivity Large, fluffy (Pattern A) Low Risk
2:1 to 3:1 Good metabolic health Mostly large particles Moderate Risk
3:1 to 5:1 Insulin resistance present Mixed, trending smaller Elevated Risk
Above 5:1 Significant insulin resistance Small, dense (Pattern B) High Risk

Note: To calculate your ratio, divide your triglyceride level by your HDL level. For example, if your triglycerides are 1.1 mmol/L and HDL is 1.3 mmol/L, your ratio is 0.85:1 (or approximately 1:1).

ApoB: A More Advanced Marker

Another marker that's gaining recognition is ApoB (Apolipoprotein B). Each LDL particle contains one ApoB molecule, so this test actually counts the number of LDL particles rather than just measuring cholesterol content.20,21

Understanding Discordance

In people with insulin resistance, you can see "discordance" between LDL-C and ApoB. For example, someone might have seemingly normal LDL cholesterol (3.1 mmol/L) but high ApoB (above 1.0 g/L), indicating they have many small, dense particles. In this case, ApoB gives you the more accurate picture of risk.20,21

What Happens to Cholesterol on a Low-Carb Diet?

Here's what we know from research: the response is highly individual and unpredictable.3,4,22,23

Three Common Patterns:

  1. LDL Decreases (30-40% of people): For many people, LDL cholesterol actually goes down on a low-carb diet, often significantly.
  2. LDL Stays the Same (30-40% of people): For others, LDL remains relatively stable.
  3. LDL Increases (20-30% of people): Some people experience an increase in LDL cholesterol, sometimes substantially.

But Here's the Key Point

Even when LDL cholesterol rises on a low-carb diet, almost everyone experiences favorable changes in other important markers:9,10,11,22

  • Triglycerides decrease (often dramatically—drops of 50% or more are common)
  • HDL cholesterol increases (the protective "good" cholesterol)
  • Triglyceride to HDL ratio improves
  • Insulin resistance decreases
  • Inflammation markers drop
  • Blood pressure often improves
  • LDL particles shift from small/dense to large/fluffy
The Net Effect: Improved Cardiovascular Risk Profile

This example shows a common scenario: LDL cholesterol rises by 23%, but the dramatic improvements in triglycerides, HDL, particle size, and insulin sensitivity result in a net reduction in cardiovascular risk. The benefits far outweigh the concern about the LDL number.

Multiple studies have shown that the metabolic improvements from a low-carbohydrate diet—particularly the reduction in insulin resistance, inflammation, and improvement in particle size—typically outweigh any increase in LDL cholesterol.9,10,22,23

The Special Case: Lean Mass Hyper-Responders

There's a specific group of people who experience very dramatic increases in LDL cholesterol on a ketogenic diet. These individuals, called "Lean Mass Hyper-Responders" (LMHR), have several distinct characteristics:24,25

  • They're typically very lean (low body fat percentage)
  • They're often very physically active or athletic
  • They follow a strict ketogenic diet
  • Their LDL cholesterol can rise to very high levels (6.5-10.3 mmol/L or higher)
  • Despite high LDL, they have excellent metabolic markers (very low triglycerides, high HDL, low insulin, no insulin resistance)

Understanding the LMHR Phenomenon

The prevailing theory is that when lean, metabolically healthy people are in deep ketosis, their bodies increase fat transport in the bloodstream to meet high energy demands. Since LDL particles carry triglycerides (a form of fat), the body produces more LDL particles to transport this fuel.24,25

Should You Be Concerned?

The significance of very high LDL in Lean Mass Hyper-Responders is an area of active research and debate. Current evidence suggests:24,25

  • These individuals typically have large, fluffy LDL particles
  • They show no other cardiovascular risk factors
  • Some studies show normal or even improved coronary artery calcium scores
  • The pattern appears to be a metabolic adaptation, not a disease state

Not Likely Your Situation

It's important to note that Lean Mass Hyper-Responders are relatively rare—most people starting a low-carb diet to address insulin resistance and weight concerns don't fit this profile. If you have excess body fat and insulin resistance, you're not a Lean Mass Hyper-Responder, and this extreme LDL elevation is unlikely to occur.24,25

If It Does Happen

For those rare individuals who do develop very high LDL levels on a ketogenic diet, the solution is straightforward: slightly increasing carbohydrate intake (while still keeping carbs relatively low) typically brings LDL back down while maintaining most of the metabolic benefits.24

The Solution: Mediterranean-Style Ketogenic Diet

If you're concerned about cholesterol levels while still wanting to address insulin resistance, there's an excellent middle ground: the Mediterranean-style low-carbohydrate or ketogenic diet.26,27,28

Why Mediterranean-Style?

The Mediterranean diet is one of the most well-researched dietary patterns for cardiovascular health. Major studies like PREDIMED have shown significant reductions in heart attacks and strokes.29,30

When you combine Mediterranean principles with carbohydrate restriction, you get:26,27,28

  • The insulin-resistance-reversing benefits of low-carb eating
  • The cardiovascular protection of Mediterranean fats and foods
  • LDL cholesterol levels that typically remain stable or decrease

Key Principles of Mediterranean-Style Low-Carb Eating:

  1. Emphasize olive oil as your primary fat source (instead of butter or other saturated fats)
  2. Eat fatty fish regularly (salmon, sardines, mackerel) for omega-3 fatty acids
  3. Include nuts and seeds daily (walnuts, almonds, chia seeds, flaxseeds)
  4. Load up on non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, peppers, tomatoes, broccoli, cauliflower)
  5. Use herbs and spices liberally (garlic, oregano, basil, turmeric)
  6. Choose quality protein sources (fish, seafood, poultry, eggs, Greek yogurt)
  7. Limit red meat and processed meats
  8. Avoid refined carbohydrates and sugars (bread, pasta, rice, sweets)

The Best of Both Worlds

This approach addresses insulin resistance while incorporating the heart-protective elements of the Mediterranean diet. Research shows this combination:26,27,28

  • Reverses insulin resistance as effectively as other low-carb approaches
  • Keeps LDL cholesterol stable or reduces it
  • Dramatically improves triglycerides and HDL
  • Reduces inflammation
  • May provide additional cardiovascular protection
  • And importantly—will keep your cardiologist happy!

Our Monitoring and Treatment Strategy

We want to be clear about our approach to your care:

1. We'll Monitor Your Complete Profile

Rather than focusing solely on LDL cholesterol, we'll track:31

  • Complete lipid panel (including triglycerides and HDL)
  • Triglyceride to HDL ratio
  • Fasting insulin and glucose
  • Hemoglobin A1c (average blood sugar)
  • Advanced markers (private pay options):
    • ApoB (Apolipoprotein B)
    • hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein for inflammation)

Note: While LDL particle size testing would be ideal, it is not currently available in Kelowna. However, we can infer your particle pattern from your triglyceride to HDL ratio and other metabolic markers with good accuracy.

2. We'll Evaluate Your True Risk

We'll look at the complete picture, not just one number. This includes:31

  • Your metabolic health markers
  • LDL particle size patterns
  • Overall cardiovascular risk factors
  • Family history
  • Lifestyle factors

3. We'll Consider Medication When Appropriate

For patients who remain at high cardiovascular risk despite dietary improvements, we may recommend medication. This is particularly important for:32,33

  • Patients with established heart disease
  • Those with very high ApoB or persistently small, dense LDL particles
  • Patients with genetic conditions affecting cholesterol (like familial hypercholesterolemia)
  • Those with multiple cardiovascular risk factors

Medication Doesn't Mean Failure

If medication is recommended, it doesn't mean your diet isn't working. Statins and other cholesterol medications work by different mechanisms than diet. They can help stabilize existing arterial plaque and reduce inflammation in blood vessel walls—benefits that complement dietary improvements rather than replace them.32,33

Think of it this way: diet addresses the root cause (insulin resistance), while medication provides additional protection for your arteries. You're getting the best of both approaches.

Putting It All Together: A Balanced Perspective

Here's what you need to remember about low-carbohydrate diets and heart health:

  1. Insulin resistance is a major driver of cardiovascular disease—often more important than LDL cholesterol alone. A low-carb diet directly addresses this root cause.5,6,7
  2. Not all LDL cholesterol is equal. Small, dense LDL particles (found in insulin-resistant people) are much more dangerous than large, fluffy particles (found in metabolically healthy people).12,13,14
  3. The triglyceride to HDL ratio is often a better predictor of cardiovascular risk than LDL cholesterol alone.17,18,19
  4. LDL response to low-carb diets is unpredictable, but most people see dramatic improvements in other important markers that typically outweigh any LDL increase.3,4,22,23
  5. For the vast majority of people, the Lean Mass Hyper-Responder phenomenon is not relevant. If you have insulin resistance and excess body weight, you're unlikely to experience extreme LDL elevation.24,25
  6. A Mediterranean-style low-carb diet offers the best of both worlds—reversing insulin resistance while keeping cholesterol in a range that won't cause concern.26,27,28,29
  7. We're monitoring all relevant markers, not just LDL. We'll work with you to assess your true cardiovascular risk and make informed decisions about treatment.31
  8. Medication remains an option when appropriate and can work synergistically with dietary improvements to provide comprehensive cardiovascular protection.32,33

The Bottom Line

Don't let fear about cholesterol numbers prevent you from addressing the underlying problem of insulin resistance. Work with your healthcare team to monitor the complete picture of your cardiovascular health. In most cases, the metabolic improvements from a well-formulated low-carbohydrate diet—especially a Mediterranean-style approach—will significantly reduce your true cardiovascular risk, regardless of what happens to your LDL cholesterol number.

Your heart health is determined by many factors working together, not by a single number on a lab report. We're here to help you navigate this journey and make the best decisions for your long-term health.
